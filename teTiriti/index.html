The Treaty of Waitangi:
Te Tiriti o Waitangi

On the 6th of February 1840 the Treaty of Waitangi was signed by 40 rangatira at Waitangi. In the following months more than 500 rangatira - including a number of women signed the treaty. The treaty resulted in the declaration of British sovereignty over New Zealand.


William Hobson

In 1839 the British government ordered naval captain William Hobson to negotiate for the sovereignty of all or parts of New Zealand to be handed to the crown and to establish a British colony. While he was en route he was advised by George Gipps - the governor of New South Wales about drafting a treaty with Māori.


The Drafting of the Treaty

William Hobson arrived in the bay of islands on 29th January 1840 and immediately set to work. Along with his secretary James Freeman he drew up some notes for a treaty before handing them off to James Busby who tidied them up and added to the. Over a single evening the treaty was translated by missionary Henry Williams and his son Edward. William Hobson presented the treaty to 500 Māori invited to Waitangi on 5 February. That night the rangatira gathered at Te Tii near the Waitangi river to decide if they would sign.


The Signing of the Treaty

On the 6th of February 1840 40 rangatira signed the Māori version of the treaty. Over the following 7 months over 500 rangatira signed - including a number of wāhine rangatira.


Lost in Translation

Likely because of the hasty translation of the document, there was a major difference between the Māori version of the treaty and the English version. The Maori version gave the queen complete governance over their land, while the English version gave the queen 'all the rights and powers of sovereignty' these differences led to much debate over the meaning - The crown thought it could do whatever it wanted while the Māori thought they still had sovereignty over their land.


The Waitangi Tribunal

In an attempt to reconcile the differences between the texts the Treaty of Waitangi act 1975 created the Waitangi Tribunal. the purpose of the Tribunal was to examine and report on proposed legislation that is reffered to them by the house of representatives or the Minister of the Crown, and to make recommendation and determinations about land transferred to the crown's ownership after the treaty.
