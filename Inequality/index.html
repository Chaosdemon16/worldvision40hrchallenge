Economic Inequality

Economic Inequality is defined as the unequal distribution of income and oppurtunity between different groups in society. Every country has it, it is a key part of capitalism but some countries have less, while others have more.


Gini Coefficient

There are many ways to measure economic inequality but the most widely used is the Gini Coefficient. The Gini Coefficient is a scale from 0-100 where 0 is complete equality and 100 is maximal inequality where 1 person has all the income while others have none. Norway has the lowest inequality with just 22.7 whilst South Africa has a Gini Coefficient of 63!


Income Tax

One way to mitigate inequality is by taxing the rich at higher rates than the poor. New Zealand's income tax system does just that. It is what is known as a progressive tax system where as your income increases so does your taxes, but your taxes do not increase on the income below the milestone. if you earn $14k you will be taxed at 10.5% but then if you earn an extra $1 your income will increase to $14,001 but the original $14k will still be taxed at 10.5%, but the extra $1 will be taxed at a rate of 17.5%. This means if you get a payrise you won't be left with less income than before because your taxes went up.


Wealth and Capital Gains Tax

The problem with income tax is that the richest people don't earn income like normal, instead they earn money from stocks and properties. These don't fall under income tax which means that the rich do not get taxed as much as the general population, worsening inequality. To resolve this issue another tax is needed - Wealth tax, and capital gains tax. New Zealand does not have one. Implementing a wealth and a capital gains tax would greatly reduce inequality.
