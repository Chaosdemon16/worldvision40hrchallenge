Nuclear Free Aotearoa

In 1987 the Labour government passed the New Zealand Nuclear Free Zone, Disarmament, and Arms Control Act cementing in law New Zealand's stance on nuclear technology and forever Aotearoa nuclear free.


Nuclear Testing in the Pacific

From 1966 New Zealand was involved in protests against french nuclear testing in the pacific, specifically around the Mururoa (or Moruroa) Atoll. In 1972 Greenpeace ships sailed to the test site and the following year in May the New Zealand and Australian governments took the French government to the international court of justice to force them to ban the test. After the France ignored the court's ruling Norman Kirk and the third labour government sent Fraser Colman along with two navy frigates the HMNZS Otago and Canterbury to the Atoll. American ship visits

After the nuclear-powered frigate USS Texas visited NZ in 1983 there were widespread protest throughout the country. At the next election the issue of nuclear ship visits was a prominent issue. The USS Texas wasn't the first however, two nuclear-powered cruisers the USS Truxton and the USS Long Beach had also attracted protest when they visited in 1976. Because the Americans had a policy of 'neither confirm nor deny' public opinion was increasingly in favour of outright banning these visits.


The Rainbow Warrior

In 1985, Aotearoa was positioned as the leader of the anti-nuclear movement. Just before midnight on the 10th of July two explosions ripped through the hull of the Greenpeace flagship the Rainbow Warrior which was moored at the Marsden Wharf in Auckland. Fernando Pereira who was a member of the crew who had been involved in protests against French nuclear tests was killed in the explosions. As it turned out two DGSE officers Dominique Prieur and Alain Mafart were responsible for the bombing on behalf of the french government. The New Zealand population was outraged and began boycotting French imports. Meanwhile, France used its influence to threaten New Zealand's access to the important market of the European Economic Community.


Nuclear-free Legislation

In late 1984 when the United states requested for the ageing guided-missile destroyer USS Buchanan visit New Zealand the Prime minister at the time David Lange stated that 'Whatever the truth of its armaments, its arrival in New Zealand would be seen as a surrender by the government.' Finally in 1987 New Zealand was officially Nuclear-free.
